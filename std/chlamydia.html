All you need to know about chlamydia

Chlamydia is a widespread bacterial infection that’s transmitted sexually. Each year, over 1.7 million chlamydia cases are reported in the United States.

Chlamydia can affect the genital, oral, and anal areas and causes serious reproductive health problems if left untreated.

It’s important to recognize the symptoms and signs of this curable condition and get the correct, one-time treatment.

The information on this page will help a non-physician see and think like a doctor. It should help you understand how doctors go about diagnosing a patient’s problem. Of course, it isn’t meant to replace a visit to a doctor or testing.

This page divided into multiple sections so it’s easy to go straight to the information you need based on the disease stage you’re in.

Each section begins with important tables that give a condensed overview of the most important facts in the section. This is followed by frequently asked questions answered by an expert STD doctor (“Dr. F”).

What’s the difference between symptoms and signs?

It’s important to distinguish symptoms from signs. Symptoms are subjective, meaning they are your sensations and experiences. Signs are objective features of the disease that can be detected not only by you but also by your doctor. Therefore, signs are more reliable than symptoms. Nevertheless, we still consider symptoms in making a diagnosis, and they are important because in the early stages of the disease you may experience only symptoms without any signs—signs may develop later.

How common are chlamydia symptoms?

According to various scientific sources, 10%–50% of males and 5%–30% of females who are infected with chlamydia develop symptoms, which means that most infected people don’t have any symptoms.

In most cases, patients are not even aware that they are infected, and they can pass the infection to their sexual partners unintentionally.

What are the common chlamydia symptoms and complications?

Here we’ll talk about common chlamydia symptoms that most people get if they have any symptoms. Please keep in mind that most people never have any symptoms.

First, let’s understand how the infection is contracted, and then we’ll cover different organs and their symptoms.

How the infection is contracted

Chlamydia bacteria come into contact with the mucosa (inner lining) of an organ and begin the invasion process. Since multiple organs can be affected, we’ll mention symptoms for each of them.

Females

In females, chlamydia first invades the cervix of the uterus and causes inflammation (cervicitis) followed by pain, vaginal itching, and painful periods. The urethra may also be involved in the inflammatory process, causing burning and pain during urination and frequent, urgent urination. Symptoms appear 5–14 days after exposure. Early on, the symptoms are usually mild, but they progress over time. All these symptoms can occur together, or there can be variations depending on which organ or organ part is affected.

Complications.When untreated, in 10%–15% of cases the inflammatory process progresses and involves the uterus, fallopian tubes, and ovaries. This condition is called pelvic inflammatory disease (PID). The person might experience pelvic or lower abdominal pain and malaise.

Oral chlamydia symptoms

Rectal chlamydia manifests with inflammation of the anus and rectum (proctitis). The person might have anal pain, anal itch, rectal pain, and/or tenesmus (a sensation of incomplete emptying of the bowel). Symptoms vary depending on the infective strain. Inflammation of the rectum can be associated with various chlamydia strains. Lymphogranuloma venerum (LGV) strains are rather common in HIV-positive heterosexual males.

How common are asymptomatic (no symptoms) chlamydia cases?

Asymptomatic cases are quite common.

Males.

Statistics differ, but many sources indicate that 50% of males are asymptomatic. According to other sources, including the CDC, this number is as high as 90%. For instance, inflammation of the urethra (urethritis) may occur without symptoms in 40%–96% of cases.2

Females.

Among infected females, 70% to 95% have no symptoms.1

Oral.

People with oral chlamydia usually have no symptoms.

Rectal.

When we talk about symptomatic or asymptomatic rectal infection, we should first consider what chlamydial strain is involved. In males (predominantly HIV-positive gay and bisexual males) and women, chlamydia may present as lymphogranuloma venerum (LGV), which can cause a variety of symptoms. Only 5% of people with the LGV form of chlamydia are asymptomatic (do not have symptoms). With chlamydial proctitis (inflammation of the rectum) caused by non-LGV strains, about 84% of infected people are asymptomatic.

What are the most reliable chlamydia symptoms? Why?

The most reliable symptoms are those a doctor trusts the most in establishing a diagnosis because they better explain the pathophysiology (how the disease evolves over time).

Penile chlamydia. The most reliable symptom is the sensation of pain and burning during urination. This discomfort is caused by inflammation in the urethra.

Vaginal chlamydia. Painful intercourse and bleeding after sex are the most reliable symptoms. These symptoms develop due to inflammation of the vagina. In the case of pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), abdominal and pelvic pain are considered reliable symptoms because they are quite common.

Oral chlamydia. Pain when swallowing food is connected with inflammation of the pharynx and is rather commonly seen in patients who are symptomatic, so it is a relatively reliable symptom.

Rectal chlamydia.Rectal pain, especially during defecation, and bleeding are connected with inflammation of the rectum and are the most reliable symptoms

How important are chlamydia symptoms in establishing the diagnosis?

Symptoms alone are not sufficient. One can suspect chlamydia but never be sure without testing

How soon do people begin experiencing symptoms?

Symptoms may occur as early as five days after exposure, but more commonly they occur after two or three weeks.

What does resolution of my symptoms mean?

The resolution of symptoms doesn’t mean that you’re fully recovered. If left untreated, chlamydia may remain dormant in affected organs for several months or even years. Sometimes the infection goes away without treatment, but we lack precise data on how often and why that happens. In some studies, it was found that chlamydia clears more slowly in males than in females (2.84 and 1.35 years, respectively). Even when dormant, the infection can lead to complications, so it is always a good idea to get tested.

Could my symptoms be psychological? How can I tell psychological symptoms apart from symptoms caused by physical disease?

We assume that your symptoms are psychological only if the disease is ruled out by testing. We always look for scientific evidence and facts. Since medicine is a science, symptoms follow patterns and rules and tend to be predictable and logical.

“Logical” means that symptoms follow a certain pattern. For instance, they should appear within a certain period of time (five days to three weeks after exposure, in the case of chlamydia). If you experience symptoms immediately after sexual intercourse, we may question whether they are caused by an infection since there should be an incubation period before symptoms develop.

“Predictable” means we should be able to predict what will elicit symptoms. Because chlamydia inflames certain parts of the body (the urethra, rectal mucosa, etc.), the pain should be caused by irritation (use) of that particular area; for instance, by urination if the urethra is involved in the process. In this example, every time (or at least most times) that you urinate, you should experience discomfort. If you have pain during urination only at lunchtime, that sounds quite uncommon and we would question it.

We carefully consider your sexual history. There is a high chance of a psychological component if you have had a new partner (whom you do not trust), tried something new with an existing partner, or have health anxiety in general. Things that you have never tried (or rarely tried) can be stressful for your brain. When stressed, people may start reading about the disease and looking for symptoms. It’s difficult to put all your symptoms into context when you are stressed. The context, in this case, includes the following: When was the contact? Has enough time passed before symptoms appeared? Do symptoms present regularly? Do they have a logical progression? Are they predictable?

Meanwhile, we never forget that diseases can have unusual and uncommon presentations, so we try to analyze all the symptoms systematically and in combination with the laboratory findings. Symptoms alone are not sufficient for making a diagnosis. We can only make an assumption by putting symptoms into context. To distinguish between psychological and non-psychological causes, we suggest testing to exclude or establish probable infection.

Chlamydia discharge

Here we will describe chlamydia discharge when the patient has no other conditions.*

Color

Chlamydia discharge is usually colorless to white.

Consistency

It’s usually very thin and watery. If the consistency is thicker or changing over time, most likely there’s another STD present as well.

Amount

Chlamydia infection does not cause significant inflammation, so the discharge is scanty.

  • Males usually see minimal discharge first thing in the morning.
  • Females might not notice it at all; they’re usually diagnosed during a gynecological exam or testing.
Smell

Chlamydia discharge usually doesn’t smell unless another condition is also present.

How are chlamydia signs used in establishing the diagnosis?

Signs are essential to making a diagnosis. They enable us to choose the most accurate testing methods and to initiate treatment while awaiting test results. In general, they give us the right vector, so we understand in which direction we should go to evaluate further. However, we cannot establish the final diagnosis based only on the signs of disease. We consider them in combination with laboratory findings before establishing the diagnosis.

Symptoms are subjective. They are sensations and experiences—you feel them rather than see them. Signs, on the other hand, are objective features of the disease you can see them. And your healthcare provider can see them too. Therefore, signs are more reliable than symptoms.

Both symptoms and signs should be analyzed in light of relevant circumstances:

⦁ How risky was the contact?
⦁ How much time passed before you felt symptoms?
⦁ Before you saw signs?
⦁ Are the symptoms logical and predictable?
⦁ Are the signs and symptoms progressing?

We can make educated assumptions based on symptoms and signs, but we can never establish the exact diagnosis based solely on them. Our assumptions are important because they allow us to start the treatment process without delay. But testing is always necessary and advised.

What are the most reliable signs of chlamydia?

We rely on signs that are either commonly seen or very specific to the disease and that have a clear, known explanation of their cause. When both of these criteria are met, a sign is referred to as reliable. Here are the most reliable signs for males and females and in the oral and rectal areas.

Males (genital)

The most reliable sign in a male is discharge from the penis, which can be yellowish, cloudy, milky, or transparent. It is caused by inflammation of the urethra.

Females (genital)

The most reliable signs in a female are:

⦁ scanty discharge from the vagina
⦁ bleeding after sexual intercourse
⦁ bleeding between periods

All these signs are explained by inflammation of the cervix (the part of the uterus that’s connected to the innermost part of the vagina)

Oral

You will usually notice no signs of oral chlamydia. We consider redness of the throat and tonsils to be the most reliable signs of the disease. They sometimes appear due to tissue damage caused by inflammation of the pharynx.

Rectal

In the case of proctitis (inflammation of the rectum), the most reliable signs are discharge and bleeding, which are caused by the inflammatory process in the rectal mucosa.

FAQ:

Is one gender more likely to contract chlamydia than the other?

Females are twice as likely to get chlamydia as men. This is explained by the fact that they have a bigger genital mucosal area and a greater chance of sustaining microscopic damage during sex.

What is the chance of getting chlamydia in one unprotected sexual act?

We lack precise data—the transmission rate per unprotected sexual act ranges from 4.5% to 40%.

How contagious is chlamydia?

  • A person can pass chlamydia as soon as he or she is infected.
  • Chlamydia patient are infectious whether or not they have symptoms.
  • Chlamydia patients continue to transmit the infection until they are cured (seven days after finishing the treatment) regardless of the presence or absence of symptoms and signs.

How is chlamydia transmitted? Why are certain organs infected more than others?

How transmission occurs

Body fluid containing chlamydia must come in contact with a specific type of tissue for infection to occur. That tissue can be found in the cervix, urethra, rectum, and eyes.

Chlamydia is a sexually transmitted disease. It can be passed through vaginal, penile, and anal contact; through oral sex (this is less risky), and by using sex toys. It is transmitted by body fluids under specific conditions.

Why some areas are more susceptible to chlamydia infection than others

Rectal, cervical, and urethral chlamydia are more common than oral chlamydia. This difference might be associated with:

  • Differences in the tissues lining these organs (chlamydia adheres to some of them more easily than others)
  • Local immune response
  • The prevalence of microlesions in the vaginal, penile, or anal area because of friction might be key

Is it possible for chlamydia to be transmitted in unusual ways?

Chlamydia cannot survive in dry conditions, so toilet seats and cutlery are not contagious

It’s not possible to get chlamydia from kissing.

If body fluids have not dried (such as on wet towels) and your genital area or eyes are exposed to them, you can get chlamydia that way.

What is the chance of a male passing chlamydia to a female, and vice versa?

Medicine lacks precise data on chlamydia transmission rates. Statistics vary. Here’s the most we can say:

Per sexual act

Male to female: 4.5%–40%

Female to male: 4.5%–32%

Multiple sexual contacts

One cross-sectional study where couples had multiple sexual contacts found

  • 7% of female partners of infected men tested positive for chlamydia
  • 76% of male partners of infected women tested positive for chlamydia

What is the chance of passing chlamydia rectally?

It is possible to pass and contract chlamydia anally

Heterosexual females

In one study, the prevalence of rectal chlamydia in heterosexual females was 11.8%.

Homosexual males

A small study found that 47% of male partners of infected gay and bisexual men had rectal chlamydia.

Overall, the risk of passing chlamydia from urethra to rectum is higher (47%) than the risk of passing it from rectum to urethra (30%). Again, this is explained by the type of tissue lining each organ and amount of microscopic damage that might occur during intercourse.

What is the chance of passing chlamydia orally?

The chance of either passing or acquiring chlamydia orally is low, but it is possible. According to one study, the prevalence of oral chlamydia is:

  • 2% in heterosexual women
  • 1.4% in gay and bisexual men

Chlamydia testing: types, accuracy, timing, and necessity :

In this chapter, we will talk about chlamydia testing

Testing is ultimately necessary to establish or rule out a diagnosis of chlamydia. All the other things we covered in earlier chapters, such as

  • Symptoms (what you feel),
  • Signs (what you see), and
  • Statistics (the odds of getting chlamydia),

help us go in the right direction with testing and early treatment while we’re waiting for the test results, but they don’t establish the diagnosis

Unlike other infections, chlamydia, if contracted, stays at the point of initial contact, so a negative test of one area of the body doesn’t rule out the possibility of having chlamydia in another area. This means that all “susceptible” areas that came into contact with the partner’s potentially affected areas during sex should be tested.

Who should be tested for chlamydia?

Chlamydia is an extremely common condition and it often has no noticeable signs or symptoms. That is why chlamydia testing is recommended for both symptomatic and asymptomatic people who fall into any of the following categories:

How are chlamydia test results interpreted?

We will talk about three different scenarios for chlamydia test results and what they mean.

  1. Positive chlamydia test

    2. If a chlamydia test (swab or urine) is positive, you are considered positive and need to be treated, and your partner needs to be notified.

  2. Negative chlamydia test

    3. If a chlamydia test is performed and is negative, that generally means you do not have chlamydia.

    But two things need to be considered

    • Timing of the test. Has enough time passed after a sexual encounter for the test to be most accurate? Two to three weeks post exposure is the ideal time to get tested.
    • The overall accuracy of the particular test performed: 98.3% accuracy for the urine test versus 94.3% accuracy for the swab test.
  3. Inconclusive or equivocal chlamydia test

    4. If the test results are not conclusive, the test needs to be repeated.

    For all inconclusive tests:

    • the ideal test needs to be performed (swab)
    • after the ideal incubation period (2–3 weeks)
    • both an alternative test and the test that had inconclusive results should be performed

Should I have a urethral swab test or urine test?

A chlamydia test is point-of-contact test.

Here, we are comparing the swab test and the urine test for urethral chlamydia only. The oral and rectal areas can be tested only with a swab test.

Both the swab test and urinalysis are widely used in the diagnosis of urethral chlamydia

Below we summarize the key points to consider when comparing the two test types and choosing between them.

  • Accuracy
    Urethral swab tests are more accurate than urine tests (99% versus 79%).
  • Pain and discomfort
    The urine test is simpler to perform since it is not invasive, and it doesn’t cause any pain.

    • Things to consider regarding the swab test

    • Males: mildly painful test; privacy issue (must expose the genitals)
    • Females: privacy issue (must expose genitals)
  • How to choose
    Screening only

    • Male: urine test

    Female: urine or swab

  • Partner is positive for chlamydia

    • Male: urine or swab

    Female: swab and urine

Since the swab test is more accurate, why is the urine test still being offered?

Though swab tests are more accurate in the detection of genital chlamydia (especially for females), sometimes people choose to take the urine test for one or more of the following reasons:

  1. The urine test is noninvasive

    2. Urine tests are noninvasive and totally free of any discomfort. By contrast, the swab test may cause mild pain for males and (rarely) vaginal bleeding during the procedure for females

  2. Cost

    3. Swab tests are more expensive than urine tests.

  3. Availability and simplicity

    4. Urine tests are easier to perform and more widely available than swab tests (you can get a urine test at almost any health facility).

How reliable (accurate) are chlamydia tests?

Chlamydia tests are considered reliable for establishing a diagnosis.

The reliability (accuracy) of the chlamydia test is based on three main factors:

  1. The type of test performed (urine vs. swab)
  2. The time that has passed from the possible chlamydia exposure (2–3 weeks after exposure is ideal)
  3. Preparation before the test (avoid douching or using vaginal creams for 24 hours, and avoid urinating for 2 hours before a urine test)
  4. The test technique—it should be properly collected.

Assuming ideal circumstances in compliance with the points above, the urine and swab tests will have 94.3% and 98.3% accuracy, respectively.

How is the swab test done in females?

The test is performed by a doctor. It takes two minutes to perform the test.

A sample of fluid from the vagina and/or the cervix will be collected by a sterile thin cotton swab (the procedure is illustrated below).

The procedure might cause very mild discomfort. If the area is infected and the mucosa is fragile, swabbing might cause minor spotting afterward (this is not common).

Remember: you should not use vaginal creams or practice douching 24 hours before being tested.

How is the swab test done in males?

A thin sterile cotton swab is inserted into the tip of the penis, three fourths of an inch into the urethra, to collect urethral fluid. The test is very quick—about 15 to 30 seconds long—and performed by a doctor. Mild discomfort at the time of the procedure is possible.

What is the swab test called?

The chlamydia swab test has several names that are used interchangeably by different labs:

  • Chlamydia NAAT test
  • Chlamydia Nucleic Acid Amplification Test
  • Chlamydia DNA probe test
  • CT TMA
  • CT Aptima

What is the urine test called?

The chlamydia urine test has a couple of interchangeable names:

  • Chlamydia NAAT test
  • Chlamydia Nucleic Acid Amplification Test

Where can I get tested for chlamydia?

Where you can get tested depends on the type of test you choose:

Urine test

  • Can be done in any health facility, including urgent care clinics, STD clinics, and the offices of primary care physicians, urologists, and gynecologists

Swab test

  • Not every health care facility carries swab kits and has a healthcare professional who performs the swab test. Swabs are mostly performed at STD clinics, Planned Parenthood offices, city sexual health clinics, and urology and gynecology clinics

Is chlamydia testing covered by health insurance?

Chlamydia testing is considered medically necessary and usually covered by health insurance. It can be done as many times as necessary.

If you want to be sure, call your insurance company and inquire about the following:

  • The preferred lab for your plan
  • If a copay, coinsurance, or deductible applies

Who should get tested for oral or rectal chlamydia?

Oral (pharyngeal) chlamydia is a common condition that affects up to 1.4% of MSMs (homosexual males) and 3%–4% of heterosexual males and females. According to international guidelines, the pharyngeal (oral) swab test for chlamydia is recommended to all persons who

  • Have oral chlamydia signs and symptoms (swollen, red pharynx; fever; pus-filled patches on the throat, etc.), and
  • Engaged in a risky behavior (oral sex)

    • According to the CDC, the oral chlamydia screening test is recommended to the following:

  • MSMs who have practiced oral sex in the preceding year. Moreover, a screening test every 3–6 months is recommended to MSMs who use methamphetamine, have sex with multiple partners, and/or have a partner who does either
  • People with HIV

Remember: chlamydia bacteria are stored locally (in mucosal cells of the area of exposure), and by getting a genital swab, urine, or blood test you cannot identify oral (pharyngeal) chlamydia.

Is retesting for genital chlamydia recommended after treatment?

No retesting after treatment is recommended. Patients with genital chlamydia who have completed the prescribed treatment and have no symptoms of infection do not need to get a test to find out if they are cured.

If you choose to get retested after the treatment

  • The kind of test used to diagnose you with chlamydia initially (urine or swab) can be done to confirm recovery. The test can be performed not earlier than 3 weeks and not later than 3 months after treatment. It is your choice to get retested for genital chlamydia recovery.

In general, people who have tested positive for chlamydia need to be tested more frequently because they are considered at higher risk to get it again. Retesting is advised every 3 months.

What type of tests are used for resistant cases?

The NAAT test is considered the gold standard for resistant chlamydia cases. This test has over 99% and 97% specificity and sensitivity, respectively, and should tell you which antibiotic is best for the chlamydia you have.


The NAAT test detects 20%–50% more chlamydial infection than a chlamydial culture test.


A strict protocol should be followed before chlamydia is considered resistant:

  1. Received the gold-standard treatment of azithromycin 1 gm once— 5.7% of chlamydia cases are resistant* to the gold-standard treatment.
  2. Waited a week after treatment before being sexually active again.
  3. Preparation before the test (avoid douching or using vaginal creams for 24 hours, and avoid urinating for 2 hours before a urine test)
  4. Avoided sex with a partner until the partner was tested and treated. The partner should be tested 2–3 weeks after the last exposure, in case infection was passed then.

*Usually, treatment failure is associated with reinfection, not antibiotic resistance.
